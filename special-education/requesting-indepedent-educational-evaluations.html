Requesting Independent Educational Evaluations (IEE)

If you disagree with the school’s evaluation, you can request an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) by a qualified examiner not employed by the district. Here’s how it works for Alabama families.

What is an IEE?

An IEE is a comprehensive evaluation conducted by an independent professional (psychologist, SLP, OT, etc.) who is not employed by the school district. Parents typically request an IEE when they believe the district’s evaluation is incomplete, inaccurate, or doesn’t reflect the student’s needs.

When parents request an IEE at public expense

Under federal special education law, if you disagree with a district evaluation, you can request an IEE at public expense. After your request, the district must, without unnecessary delay, either:

  • agree to fund the IEE under its criteria, or
  • file for due process to defend its evaluation.

“Public expense” means the district pays for the IEE or ensures it’s provided at no cost to you.

Did you know?

The school must consider the results of any IEE you share with them, whether you paid for it or the district did—though they don’t have to accept every recommendation.

When to Consider an IEE

  • The evaluation seems too brief or misses areas of concern (e.g., dyslexia, ADHD, autism, language, motor, behavior).
  • Scores don’t match what you or teachers observe day-to-day.
  • Recommended services or accommodations seem out of step with needs.
  • You want a second opinion from a specialist with deeper expertise.

How to Request an IEE (Step-by-Step)

  1. Send a written request to the principal and special education director stating you disagree with the district’s evaluation and are requesting an IEE at public expense.
  2. Ask for the district’s IEE criteria (qualifications, cost caps, location limits, timelines). You do not have to explain your disagreement in detail, but you can share concerns if you want.
  3. Wait for the district’s response. They must either agree to fund the IEE or file for due process to defend their evaluation.
  4. Choose a qualified evaluator who meets district criteria. If no one local meets a restriction, ask for a criteria waiver with a short justification.
  5. Provide consent and release so the evaluator can review records, speak with teachers, and observe if needed.
  6. Meet to review results once the IEE is complete. Request an IEP meeting to consider recommendations and update the plan as appropriate.
Sample IEE Request Email 
Subject: Request for Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at Public Expense

Dear [Principal Name] and [Special Education Director Name],

I am writing to inform you that I disagree with the district’s evaluation of my child, [Student Name], [DOB], at [School]. I am requesting an Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE) at public expense.

Please send me the district’s criteria for IEEs (evaluator qualifications, cost parameters, geographic/location limits, and timelines), as well as the process for approving evaluators and payment.

I look forward to your response. I am available at [phone/email].

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Date]

Tip: Send via email and keep a copy. If you hand-deliver, ask the office to time-stamp a copy for your records.

Who Pays? What Are the Rules?

  • District-funded IEE: If the district agrees, they typically pay the provider directly or reimburse you. They may give you a list of evaluators—but you can request anyone who meets their criteria.
  • Due process option: If the district refuses to fund, it must file for due process without unnecessary delay to show its evaluation was appropriate.
  • Parent-funded IEE: You can always obtain a private evaluation at your own expense; the district must still consider the results.
  • Reasonable criteria: District criteria (cost caps, distance) must be reasonable and not effectively block your right to an IEE.

Choosing an Evaluator

  • Look for specialty experience (e.g., dyslexia assessment, autism diagnostics, bilingual evaluation).
  • Ask about school observations, classroom strategies, and practical recommendations—not just test scores.
  • Confirm they meet district criteria; request a waiver if necessary (e.g., nearest specialist is outside the radius).
  • Ask for a written report and a parent-friendly summary page you can share with teachers.

After the IEE: Using the Results

  • Request an IEP meeting to review the IEE and update goals, services, and accommodations.
  • Ask the evaluator to attend (district-funded IEEs often include participation).
  • Translate recommendations into specific, measurable supports (service minutes, accommodation wording, progress monitoring).

Tip

Bring a one-page “action list” to the meeting: which goals/services you want added or changed and the exact wording for key accommodations.

IEE FAQs

  • Do I have to explain why I disagree? No. You may, but the district can’t require detailed reasons to process your request.
  • Can the district set a cost cap? Yes, but it must be reasonable for your area and needs; ask how they set it and request a waiver if needed.
  • Can I pick anyone? You can propose any qualified evaluator meeting district criteria; you aren’t limited to the district’s list.
  • How fast must they respond? The law says “without unnecessary delay.” If it’s dragging on, ask for timelines in writing or consider dispute resolution options.
